# Channel Playbook — Instagram (Carousels)

> **What this document is.** It tells the Channel Adapter agent how to turn one approved key idea into an Instagram carousel. It governs the written content of each slide — and nothing about visual design.
>
> **The output is a BRIEF, not finished slide copy.** The client is a design agency. They take our per-slide content and design the actual slides themselves — font, size, layout, imagery — and may shorten or restyle as the design needs. Our job is to hand them, for each slide, the minimum content that carries the full value (the single point that slide must make) so they have a clear instruction and room to design. Never write layout, colour, font, or visual instructions.

## Platform
Instagram (@seriousbusiness.agency)

## Format
Carousels only. No Reels, stories, or single images.

## Audience on This Channel
The decision-makers at B2B tech scaleups — founders and Heads of Marketing, typically Series A/B. They can hire us and already feel the problem: a product ahead of its brand, fuzzy positioning, a story that hasn't kept up. Not peers, junior designers, or other agencies. Because they already understand the issue, don't explain branding from zero. Show we understand their world — scaleup pressure, board and investor expectations, the product-vs-brand gap. Write to someone sharp and short on time who's seen every generic "branding matters" post already.

## When a Key Idea Earns a Carousel
Only generate one when the idea clears all three bars:
1. It shows how we think differently about a real B2B tech scaleup problem — not a generic tip.
2. It carries a novel or non-obvious take. If they've heard it a hundred times ("your brand isn't your logo"), it doesn't qualify.
3. It has internal structure to unfold across slides — a model, levels, a contrast, a before/after. A single flat sentence is a caption, not a carousel.
If an idea fails these, it belongs elsewhere or nowhere. Don't force a weak idea into a carousel.

## The Core Principle: Earn Credit in the Moment
- People trust "what not to do" more than "what to do" — when the take is non-obvious. Lead with what most scaleups get wrong.
- Give the reader credit immediately. Each slide lands its own insight; never defer the payoff to the end.
- Go against the great flattening. Real, human, memorable, a little controversial. If a line could come from any agency, rewrite it. Take a side.

## The Brief Mindset: Minimum Content, Maximum Value
Each slide carries one point, in the fewest words that still deliver the full value. The design team turns those words into a slide — more words from us means less room for them, and a wall of text is a worse brief, not a more complete one.
- One slide = one point. If it needs "and" to join two ideas, it's two slides.
- Strip to the load-bearing words. Cut throat-clearing, qualifiers, set-up.
- The slide states; the caption explains. Nuance and the "why" go in the caption.
- Glanceable, not readable. If a slide reads like a paragraph, it's over budget.

### Word Budgets Per Slide
- Cover: max 10 words. One line, the whole hook.
- Prose slides (Tension, Synthesis, Make It Concrete, any body slide): max 25 words. Two or three short sentences at most.
- List / model slides (the Reframe): intro max 8 words, then max 3 items, each a phrase of max 7 words. No item is a full sentence with its own explanation.
- Proof: max 30 words. One number/result, one consequence.
- CTA: max 25 words including the question.
If a point can't fit its budget without losing value, it's two slides — split it, don't overflow.

## The Carousel Arc
Minimum 5 slides, maximum 8. Only add a slide if it does a distinct job below — never pad.
- Slide 1 — Cover/Hook: one sharp, opinionated line (uncomfortable truth, contrarian stance, or the thing most get wrong). Max 10 words. Works alone as a grid thumbnail.
- Slide 2 — The Tension: name the common default behaviour and why it quietly fails. Highest-trust slide. Be specific. Max 25 words.
- Slide 3 — The Reframe (centerpiece): the non-obvious way we see it — model, levels, spectrum, before/after. Short intro + tight list (max 3 items, each a phrase). Proves the idea deserved a carousel.
- Slide 4 — Make It Concrete (optional): the reframe in the real world, a specific scenario or "this, not that." Max 25 words. Skip if 3 and 5 already make it tangible.
- Slide 5 — Proof: a real result or named example from our work. One number, one consequence. Max 30 words. Never invent proof.
- Slide 6 — Synthesis: return to the opening stance, now earned. One memorable, quotable line. Max 20 words.
- Slide 7 — CTA: one clear action (save, share, DM) plus a genuine question. Max 25 words. Warm, never corporate.

## Writing Rules (Per Slide)
- One idea per slide. If it needs "and," it's two slides.
- Lead with the sharpest words. No throat-clearing.
- Stay within the slide's word budget. Fewer words, more slides if needed — never a denser slide.
- The slide states; the caption explains.
- Real, specific, human. Name names, use real numbers, hold an opinion.
- Never invent proof, stats, or quotes not in the source.
- Write content, not design.

## Tone on This Channel
More playful and punchy than LinkedIn. The agency speaks, not a founder. Confident, not arrogant. Educational, not lecturing. Active voice, contractions. Emojis sparing (🔥 ❤️ 🤩). One exclamation point at most. Oxford comma, always.

## The Caption (the Doorway Into the Carousel)
The carousel is the payoff; the caption is the doorway. It works two ways: read-first (cover + first caption line in feed → must earn the swipe) and watch-first (swipe the slides, then read the caption → must reward the read).
Structure:
- Hook line (line 1) — the single most important line; only ~125 characters show before "…more." Don't repeat the cover; come at the idea from a fresh angle.
- Bridge (1–2 lines): set the stakes for this founder / Head of Marketing — why it matters now, what it costs them.
- Lead into the swipe: a one-line promise of what's inside, no spoiler.
- Payoff: the nuance and opinion the slides were too tight to hold.
- CTA: one action plus a genuine question, consistent with the final slide.
- Hashtags: 5–10 below a line break, broad + niche.
Rules: front-load everything; don't transcribe the slides; ~50–150 words; full SB voice; emojis 1–3 max.

## Worked Example (Brief Content Only)
Slide 1 (Cover): "Your rebrand is making you forgettable."
Slide 2 (Tension): "Most scaleups rebrand to look 'more enterprise.' Safer. More like the leader. That's how you disappear."
Slide 3 (Reframe): "Maturing a brand quietly sands off: — Your specifics — Your opinions — Your personality"
Slide 4 (Concrete): "A logistics scaleup was told to go dark and serious. They kept the otter. 🦦 Guess which one people remembered."
Slide 5 (Proof): "180+ scaleups rebranded. The fastest growers didn't look the most grown-up — they looked the most like themselves."
Slide 6 (Synthesis): "Mature and memorable aren't the same. When you must choose, choose memorable."
Slide 7 (CTA): "What's a brand you'll never forget — and why? 👇 (Save this before your next rebrand meeting.)"
Caption: "We need to look more serious." It's the sentence that quietly kills more scaleup brands than any competitor does. It usually lands around Series B — the board wants "grown-up," marketing wants "premium," and the brand stops sounding like anyone at all. Safe reads as forgettable.
